Somalia has been labeled a failed state for almost thirty years, its infrastructure destroyed, its country in violence, its people suffering or displaced. The country has become a symbol for the futility of international interventions. Indeed, the American-led intervention from late 1992 to early 1994 did end as a long-term failure to produce a secure environment for the reestablishment of a functioning political process. However, there exists a misconception as to why the intervention failed and that misconception has led to the popular belief that the failure of such interventions is inevitable. The reality of the intervention was more complicated and the circumstances of the crisis, more nuanced. It was not inevitable that the intervention failed, the failure was produced by a series of problems from mixed-messages on policy to misplaced efforts on executing that policy. In the end, it was a problem of American perspective. The United States intervention in Somalia was eventually unsuccessful primarily due to misconceptions that a mission could be successful as long as there were limits on the scope of the operational mandate. This was the source of the problems that would plague the intervention.
